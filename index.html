TL;DR

Billions of simulated steps, guided by minutes of human driving.

Pure self-play can discover effective driving policies, but those policies may be behaviorally alien around humans. The paper studies a simple alternative: train a behavioral cloning anchor on a small slice of human demonstrations, then regularize PPO self-play toward that anchor.

Across held-out Waymo Open Motion scenarios, a small anchor pulls the policy toward human-compatible behavior while preserving the scale and robustness advantages of synthetic self-play experience.

Method

A pinch of human behavior inside an ocean of self-play.

1

Learn a human anchor

Train a compact behavioral cloning policy from selected human driving demonstrations.

2

Train through self-play

Use PPO in simulation so the agent sees billions of synthetic transitions and an automatically evolving curriculum.

3

Regularize toward compatibility

Add a KL penalty from the anchor to the policy, keeping the agent effective while making its behavior easier for human drivers to coordinate with.

Abstract

Cheap simulation, small human anchor, human-compatible behavior.

Self-play reinforcement learning has recently taken the wheel in autonomous driving. This approach uses cheap, large-scale simulation to substitute expensive, large-scale human demonstration data. While cost-effective, agents trained through self-play often behave unpredictably around unseen policies and fail to coordinate with human drivers due to incompatible driving styles.

We learn driving policies by training on over 30 years of self-play simulation against a minimal reward function and just 30 minutes of human demonstrations. Our agents achieve near-zero-shot coordination with real human driving trajectories using 125,000x less human data than imitation learning baselines, at 1/25 the number of GPU hours.
